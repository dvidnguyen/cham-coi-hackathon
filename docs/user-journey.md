# User Journey Scripts — Chạm Cõi

> Page-by-page scripts for the **mentor journey**, in English.
> Grounded in the current site (Home, About, Gallery, Contact) and the journey matrix
> (Awareness → Consideration → Onboarding → Participation → Retention & Advocacy).

---

## The traveler — who the journey is for

**Primary: the Mentor.** An older Vietnamese craft-keeper — lacquer painting, wood carving, weaving, village crafts. They hold decades of lived heritage but believe "my experience isn't worth much anymore." They move within family and community circles, have low digital literacy, and rarely look for teaching opportunities online.

**Companions on the journey:**
- **The family member** (often a child or grandchild) — the digital helper who reads the screen, fills forms, and relays messages.
- **The younger learner** — the audience the mentor will eventually teach.

The arc of the whole site is one transformation:

> *From "I stay in my small circle" → to "I am a recognized, returning mentor whose craft is needed by a new generation."*

---

## Journey map → pages

| Stage | Mentor's shift | Page that carries it |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | "My experience is valuable and needed." | **Home** → **About** |
| **Consideration** | "I trust this organization and I understand what I'd get." | **About** (testimonials, impact) |
| **Onboarding** | "My family can help me register, and I know who to call." | **Contact** |
| **Participation** | "I can join a workshop and teach with confidence." | **Gallery** (workshop calendar, themes) |
| **Retention & Advocacy** | "I'm recognized, I'll come back, I'll tell others." | **Gallery** (mentor stories, recognition) |

## 1. Home — Stage: Awareness (the hook)

**Where the mentor is when they land here:** they didn't come looking to teach. A family member showed them the site, or they followed a link shared in a community group. They are curious but guarded, and they can't read small dense text easily.

**The page's job:** in the first 5 seconds, make one idea land — *what you carry is wanted here.* Don't ask for anything yet. Make them feel seen.

**Script — beats & voice**
- **Voice:** warm, unhurried, reverent. Short lines. Big, legible type. Let images do the heavy lifting (the craft is the hero, not the UI).
- **Beat 1 — Recognition.** Open on the craft and the human behind it. Headline direction: *Heritage lives in your hands.* The visitor should feel: "this is about people like me."
- **Beat 2 — The bridge.** Introduce the intergenerational idea (the existing *"Nhịp (Cầu) Giao Thoa Thời Đại" / "Moments of Connection"*). Show elders and youth in the same frame. The subtext: *you belong to a lineage that the young are searching for.*
- **Beat 3 — The invitation.** A single, gentle call: *See the workshops →* and *Read our story →*. No forms, no jargon, no urgency.
- **Beat 4 — Reassurance footer.** "Prefer to talk to a person? Zalo · Hotline." — surfaced early so the digitally hesitant know there's a human door.

**Copy to land:** one promise — *"We connect keepers of Vietnamese craft with a new generation ready to learn."*

**What's already here / gap:** Home already delivers Beats 1–3 (hero, Sector 2 bridge, Sector 3 gallery wall). **Missing:** the human contact reassurance (Zalo/hotline) in the hero/footer, and alt-text/legibility for low-vision older visitors.

## 2. About — Stage: Awareness → Consideration (trust & meaning)

**Where the mentor is:** intrigued, but skeptical. *"Is this real? What do they want from me? Will I be respected, or just used for photos?"* They need proof and meaning before they'll consider giving their time.

**The page's job:** move them from *curious* to *I trust this, and I understand what I'd gain.* Answer the three silent questions: **Is it real? Is it safe? Is it worth my time?**

**Script — beats & voice**
- **Voice:** honest, specific, human. Real names, real faces, real numbers. No marketing fog.
- **Beat 1 — Why we exist.** State the mission plainly (the existing "Why we exist" section). Frame the problem the mentor already feels: *heritage is fading because the young have no door to it.* Position the mentor as the answer, not the beneficiary.
- **Beat 2 — Social impact, made tangible.** Not "we empower communities" — instead: *47 elders mentored · 1,200 young learners · 9 craft villages.* Numbers the family member can read aloud and the mentor can be proud of.
- **Beat 3 — Cultural preservation stories.** Short, visual stories of crafts kept alive. Let the mentor recognize their own craft and think, *"that could be me."*
- **Beat 4 — Testimonials (Consideration).** Real mentors — name, craft, photo, one honest line: *"I was nervous I had nothing left to teach. The first workshop proved me wrong."* This is the single most powerful trust builder.
- **Beat 5 — Mentor benefits, plainly listed.** What they get: a small stipend, transport help, a printed teaching guide, a certificate, and recognition. No fine print hiding.
- **Beat 6 — Transparent policies.** Rights, privacy (we never share your info), no cost to join, free to stop anytime. This kills the "is it a scam?" fear.
- **Beat 7 — FAQ, in their language.** "Do I need to be good with computers?" → No. "Can my child help me register?" → Yes, please do. "What if I can't commit every week?" → That's fine.

**CTA:** *"Become a mentor →"* leading to Contact, with the family-assisted path made obvious.

**What's already here / gap:** About already has the story, values, and quotes (Beats 1, 3, and part of 4). **Missing / to strengthen:** hard impact numbers (Beat 2), the explicit mentor-benefits list (Beat 5), transparent policies (Beat 6), and a plain-language FAQ (Beat 7).

## 3. Gallery — Stage: Participation → Retention (proof & belonging)

**Where the mentor is:** they've signed up and taught once, or they're considering a first workshop. They need to *see themselves doing it* — and after they've taught, they need to *be seen.*

**The page's job:** lower the fear of the first workshop, then turn one-time mentors into proud, returning ambassadors.

**Script — beats & voice**
- **Voice:** celebratory but dignified. This is the mentor's wall of honor, not a content feed.
- **Beat 1 — The wall.** The existing gallery wall ("Moments of Connection") — real workshops, real hands at work. The subtext for a hesitant mentor: *look how ordinary and warm this is — you can do this.*
- **Beat 2 — Workshop calendar (Participation).** Upcoming sessions, each with: date, craft theme, location, and a one-line "what you'd teach." Let a mentor (or their family) pick a slot without guesswork. Predefined teaching themes remove the "what would I even say?" anxiety.
- **Beat 3 — Mentor stories.** A rotating feature: one mentor, their craft, their first-day fear, their breakthrough. A newer mentor reads it and thinks, *"that's exactly how I feel."*
- **Beat 4 — Recognition (Retention).** Photos from past classes, mentor names, thank-yous from learners. Make belonging visible — *you are part of this, and it shows.*
- **Beat 5 — The loop back.** A mentor who finishes a workshop lands here again and sees *themselves* on the wall. That moment is what makes them return and tell a friend.

**CTA:** *"Join the next workshop →"* and *"Nominate a mentor →"* (advocacy).

**What's already here / gap:** Gallery is currently a placeholder. **To build:** workshop calendar (Beat 2), mentor-story feature (Beat 3), and recognition module (Beat 4). The gallery wall component exists and can be reused.

## 4. Contact — Stage: Onboarding (the bridge across the digital gap)

**Where the mentor is:** convinced, but stuck at the screen. They can't fill a complex form alone. The moment of highest drop-off is right here — if onboarding feels cold or technical, they leave and never come back.

**The page's job:** make signing up feel like a family member holding the door open. Zero friction, zero shame, a real human one tap away.

**Script — beats & voice**
- **Voice:** patient, personal, Vietnamese-first. Speak to the family helper as much as the mentor: *"You can fill this in for them."*
- **Beat 1 — Permission to be helped.** Open with: *"A family member can complete this for you"* — said out loud, not buried. This single line saves the majority of older mentors.
- **Beat 2 — The easy door first.** Before any form: **Zalo · Hotline · Walk-in**. "Prefer to talk? We'll call you back the same day." For a low-literacy mentor, a phone call beats any form.
- **Beat 3 — A simple, family-assisted form.** Big fields, few required, plain labels: Name · Craft · Phone · "Best time to call." Optional everything else. A progress hint and a friendly success screen: *"We've got it — someone will reach out within a day."*
- **Beat 4 — What happens next.** Three plain steps: *1) We call you. 2) We match you to a workshop near you. 3) We send a printed guide.* No mystery.
- **Beat 5 — The safety repeat.** One line: *"Free to join. Free to stop. We never share your details."*

**CTA:** the form, or simply *"Call the hotline."*

**What's already here / gap:** Contact currently holds a **partnership / co-organization form** (workshop booking, sponsorship, venue, industry — aimed at organizations, not mentors). **For the mentor journey, this page needs a separate, simpler mentor-onboarding path** (Beats 1–5) alongside the existing partnership form — or the two should be clearly separated (e.g., "I'm a mentor / I'm an organization").

## Cross-page principles (apply everywhere)

- **Big type, high contrast, Vietnamese-first.** Assume tired eyes and a small screen.
- **A human is always one tap away** — Zalo and hotline visible on every page, not just Contact.
- **Show, don't tell.** Real faces and real craft beat adjectives every time.
- **Two readers in mind** — the mentor *and* the family member beside them. Write so either can act.
- **Recognition is recurring.** Every stage should leave the mentor feeling more seen than the last.
